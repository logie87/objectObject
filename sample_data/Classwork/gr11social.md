# GRADE 11 SOCIAL SCIENCES  
## INDEPENDENT ISSUE SNAPSHOT – 1 WEEK ASSIGNMENT  
Teacher + Student Markdown Edition

---

## SECTION A: TEACHER OVERVIEW

### Title
**Independent Issue Snapshot (Social Sciences 11)**

### Timeframe
**1 week**

### Purpose

A short, focused assignment to assess how students can:

- Select and investigate a **contemporary or historical social issue**.
- Summarize key facts and context accurately.
- Analyze perspectives and implications.
- Make evidence-based connections to course concepts (e.g., power, ideology, inequality, rights, globalization, environment, governance).
- Communicate clearly in one structured written product plus a brief presentation.

Designed to be:

- Light enough for one week.
- Easy to differentiate (including IEPs).
- Usable in any Grade 11 social sciences course: Social Studies, Law, Psychology, Sociology, Human Geography, etc.

### Core Components (Suggested)

1. **Issue Proposal + Check-in** – completion  
2. **Issue Snapshot Brief (written)** – 30 marks  
3. **Perspective & Impact Task** – 15 marks  
4. **Mini Presentation (Issue Pitch)** – 10 marks  

**Total Suggested:** 55 marks (adjust to your system)

### Recommended Week Flow

- **Day 1:** Introduce task, model an example, students choose issue & get approval.  
- **Day 2:** Research period + guided note-taking.  
- **Day 3:** Draft the Issue Snapshot Brief.  
- **Day 4:** Add Perspective & Impact section, refine.  
- **Day 5:** Mini presentations + submit final work.

### Submission Expectations

Students submit (digital or paper, as directed):

- Approved **Issue Topic** (Day 1/2)
- **Issue Snapshot Brief** (1–2 pages)
- **Perspective & Impact Task** (same doc or attached)
- **Mini Presentation** (live or recorded; 2–3 minutes)

### Marking Focus (Teacher Summary)

**Issue Snapshot Brief (30)**  
- Accuracy and clarity of explanation  
- Use of **at least 2 credible sources**  
- Organized, concise, readable

**Perspective & Impact (15)**  
- Identifies **at least two perspectives**  
- Describes who is affected and how  
- Links to relevant course concepts

**Mini Presentation (10)**  
- Clear, structured, under 3 minutes  
- Explains issue and why it matters  
- Accessible to a non-expert audience

### IEP / Differentiation Options

You may:

- Allow shorter briefs, bullet-point sections, or audio/visual submissions.
- Provide curated sources instead of open research.
- Focus grading more on understanding and effort than polish.
- Offer sentence stems, organizers, exemplars.
- For extension: require 3–5 sources and deeper theoretical links.

All students follow the same structure:  
**Issue → Explain → Perspectives → Impact → Communicate**

## SECTION B: STUDENT ASSIGNMENT

### Title
**Independent Issue Snapshot – Social Sciences 11**

### Time
**1 week**

You will choose a real social issue, learn about it, and explain it clearly.

You will:

1. Pick an issue and get it approved.  
2. Write an **Issue Snapshot Brief**.  
3. Add a **Perspective & Impact** section.  
4. Do a **2–3 minute mini presentation**.

Your teacher will give you the exact due dates.

---

## PART 1: CHOOSING YOUR ISSUE (CHECK-IN)

Choose a **current, recent, or historically significant** issue that connects to this course. Examples:

- Voting rights or youth political engagement  
- Housing affordability  
- Indigenous rights and land claims  
- Climate policy and resource development  
- Immigration or refugee policy  
- Criminal justice or policing  
- Mental health supports for youth  
- Media bias and misinformation  
- Gender equality or LGBTQ2S+ rights  
- Global conflict, peacekeeping, humanitarian crises

**Requirements:**

- It must be real.
- It must connect to society, power, rights, systems, or ethics.
- It must be narrow enough for a 1-week project.
- It must be **approved by your teacher**.

**Submit (Day 1/2):**

- **Issue topic:**  
- **1–2 sentences:** why you chose it / how it links to the course.

---

## PART 2: ISSUE SNAPSHOT BRIEF (30 MARKS)

**Length:** about 1–2 pages.

Use these headings:

### 1. Issue Overview

In **1–2 paragraphs**:

- What is the issue?  
- Where is it happening?  
- Who is involved?  
- Why is this an issue (what is at stake)?

### 2. Background (Key Facts)

In **3–6 bullet points or a short paragraph**:

- Key events, laws, stats, or examples.  
- Use **at least 2 credible sources**.  
- At the bottom, list your sources (simple links or titles are fine).

### 3. Course Connection

In **1 short paragraph**:

- Explain how this issue connects to what we study:
  - e.g., rights, justice, inequality, globalization, government, ideology, etc.

**Marked On (30):**

- Clear, accurate explanation of the issue.  
- Relevant background and evidence.  
- At least 2 sources used.  
- Logical structure and readable writing (minor errors are fine).

---

## PART 3: PERSPECTIVE & IMPACT TASK (15 MARKS)

Add this to the same document.

### 4. Perspectives

In **1–2 paragraphs or bullets**:

- Identify **at least two different perspectives** on this issue.
- For each perspective:
  - What do they believe or want?
  - Why do they see it that way?

### 5. Who Is Affected?

In **1 short paragraph or bullet list**:

- Who is most affected (groups or communities)?
- How are they impacted?
  - (social, economic, political, environmental, cultural, etc.)

**Marked On (15):**

- Shows more than one side.  
- Clearly explains who is affected and how.  
- Connects to course ideas (e.g., power, equity, rights, systems).

---

## PART 4: MINI PRESENTATION – “WHY THIS ISSUE MATTERS” (10 MARKS)

**Length:** 2–3 minutes.

Format: live to class, small group, or recorded (ask your teacher).

Include:

1. Issue name and location.  
2. Short explanation of what is happening.  
3. One key perspective (what one group believes).  
4. Who is affected.  
5. In 1–2 sentences, why people your age should care.

You may use:

- A small cue card
- A simple slide
- Your written brief as notes

**Marked On (10):**

- Clear and organized  
- Understandable for someone new to the topic  
- Stays within time, stays on topic
